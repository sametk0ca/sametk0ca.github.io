# Concept: Ethics in Cyber Security

## Overview
Cyber security practitioners hold positions of asymmetric power and trust. Ethical norms guide behavior where laws may be insufficient or silent.

## Key Ethical Challenges
1. **Client Obligations**: Balancing confidentiality with the duty to report wrongdoing (e.g., if a client's actions harm third parties).
2. **Asymmetric Knowledge**: Ensuring that technical expertise is not used to exert undue influence or harm.
3. **Whistle-blowing**: The difficult decision to report system risks or illicit activity, weighed against the harm of "capricious or misguided" reporting.

## Codes of Conduct
- **ACM Code of Ethics**: A global standard for computing professionals, emphasizing the avoidance of harm and the importance of professional responsibility.
- **CREST Code of Conduct**: Specifically for firms/individuals in penetration testing, focusing on service methodology and business ethics.

## Vulnerability Disclosure
Ethical researchers often follow **"Responsible Disclosure"**:
1. **Private Disclosure**: Notify the vendor/owner first to allow for remediation.
2. **Embargo Period**: Wait a pre-agreed time before making the information public.
3. **Public Disclosure**: Inform the community to encourage broader security and learning.

*Note: Monetizing vulnerabilities (e.g., bug bounties) is generally accepted if done through authorized programs, but "extortion-like" demands are unethical and often illegal.*
