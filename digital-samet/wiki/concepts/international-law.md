# Concept: Public International Law in Cyberspace

## Overview
Public International Law regulates relationships between states. It is now widely accepted that these principles apply to cyberspace, with the **Tallinn Manual 2.0** serving as the primary academic restatement.

## Key Principles
1. **Territorial Sovereignty**: States are sovereign within their territory. A cyber operation by one state that interferes with the internal affairs of another can be a violation of sovereignty.
2. **Attribution**: The process of determining if a state is legally responsible for a cyber act (e.g., acts by its military or non-state actors under its direction).
3. **Use of Force**: A cyber operation can constitute a "use of force" (or even an "armed attack") if its scale and effects are comparable to a kinetic attack.
4. **Countermeasures**: A state harmed by another state's illegal cyber act can take proportionate countermeasures (which can be cyber or kinetic).

## Specific Scenarios
- **Cyber Espionage**: Generally *not* a violation of international law during peacetime, though it may violate domestic laws.
- **Law of Armed Conflict (LOAC)**: In times of war, cyber operations must follow principles like:
    - **Military Necessity**: Force used must be necessary for victory.
    - **Distinction**: Attacks must distinguish between military targets and civilians.
    - **Proportionality**: Collateral damage must not be excessive relative to the military gain.
