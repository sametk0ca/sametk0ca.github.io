# Concept: Privacy Paradigms

## Overview
Privacy is a multi-dimensional concept that can be interpreted through different technical lenses. Each paradigm leads to different types of privacy-preserving technologies.

## 1. Privacy as Confidentiality
- **Goal**: Minimize the amount of personal information exposed to any entity, especially unauthorized third parties.
- **Scope**: Includes both data (content) and **metadata** (identity, frequency of use, location).
- **Techniques**: End-to-end encryption (E2EE), obfuscation, and inference control.
- **Reference**: [[privacy-as-confidentiality]]

## 2. Privacy as Informational Control
- **Goal**: Provide users with the means to decide what information they expose to others.
- **Context**: Often referred to as "the right to informational self-determination."
- **Techniques**: Privacy settings, preference expression, and policy negotiation.
- **Reference**: [[informational-control]]

## 3. Privacy as Transparency
- **Goal**: Inform users about what data they have exposed and who has accessed or processed it.
- **Context**: Enabling accountability and awareness of one's digital footprint.
- **Techniques**: Secure logging, digital footprint visualization, and data access notifications.
- **Reference**: [[privacy-as-transparency]]

## Contextual Integrity
A key concept in privacy design is **Contextual Integrity (Nissenbaum)**. It posits that information flow is not inherently private or public, but its appropriateness depends on:
- **Actors**: Who is sending and receiving?
- **Information Types**: What is being sent?
- **Transmission Principles**: Under what terms is the information being sent?
