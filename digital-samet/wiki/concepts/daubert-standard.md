# Daubert Standard

- **Source:** [[ka-forensics]]
- **Tags:** #forensics #legal #standard

## Overview
The Daubert Standard is a legal rule used in the United States to determine the admissibility of expert witness testimony and scientific evidence in legal proceedings. It originated from three US Supreme Court cases in the 1990s (Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, etc.).

## Admissibility Criteria
Trial judges act as "gatekeepers" and evaluate forensic evidence based on four basic criteria:

1. **Testability:** The theoretical underpinnings of the methods must yield testable predictions (falsifiability).
2. **Peer Review:** The methods should preferably be published in a peer-reviewed journal.
3. **Error Rate:** There should be a known rate of error for evaluating results.
4. **General Acceptance:** The methods should be generally accepted within the relevant scientific community.

## Context
- **Global Influence:** While originating in the US, these principles have been broadly accepted in other jurisdictions, including the UK (e.g., Law Commission proposal No. 190).
- **Flexibility:** The standards are intended to be flexible, giving judges leeway in determining admissibility.

## Comparison
- **Frye Standard (1923):** The predecessor to Daubert, which focused solely on "general acceptance" within the scientific community.
