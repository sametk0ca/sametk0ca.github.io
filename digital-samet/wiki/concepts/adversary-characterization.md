# Concept: Adversary Characterization

## Overview
Adversaries are categorized by their motivations, capabilities, and the nature of their crimes. A fundamental distinction is made between cyber-enabled and cyber-dependent crimes.

## 1. Cyber-Enabled vs. Cyber-Dependent
- **Cyber-Enabled Crimes**: Traditional crimes (e.g., fraud, stalking, drug dealing) that use the Internet to increase reach, speed, and anonymity.
- **Cyber-Dependent Crimes**: Crimes that can only be committed using computers or networks (e.g., hacking, DDoS, malware creation).

## 2. Interpersonal Offenders
Individual or small-group actions often driven by personal, social, or malicious intent:
- **Cyberbullying & Doxing**: Harassment and public release of private information to embarrass or silence victims.
- **Cyberstalking**: Using electronic means to monitor or interact with victims against their will.
- **Sextortion**: Threatening to release intimate images unless a payment is made.
- **Child Predation**: Grooming and sharing illicit material, often facilitated by anonymizing technologies like Tor.

## 3. Motivations
- **Financial**: Pro-active organized crime seeking maximum profit with minimum risk.
- **Political**: Hacktivists seeking to spread a message or protest.
- **Strategic/National**: State actors seeking espionage, sabotage, or disinformation.
- **Personal/Emotional**: Interpersonal offenders driven by revenge, obsession, or social dynamics.

## Key Incentives for Online Crime
- **Reach**: Easier to find and contact victims globally.
- **Cost**: Operations are cheap (e.g., mass email is free).
- **Speed**: Immediate execution compared to physical counterparts.
- **Anonymity**: Difficult for law enforcement to track across international boundaries.
