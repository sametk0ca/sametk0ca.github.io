# Concept: Privacy and Democratic Values

## Overview
Privacy technologies are essential for protecting civil liberties and supporting democratic political systems. They prevent mass manipulation and allow for the free expression of opinions.

## 1. Electronic Voting (eVoting)
Systems designed to enable fair elections in adversarial conditions.
- **Ballot Secrecy**: Using **Mix Networks** or **Homomorphic Encryption** to ensure a vote cannot be linked to a voter.
- **Universal Verifiability**: Anyone can verify that the tally is correct without knowing individual votes (using Zero-Knowledge Proofs).
- **Eligibility**: Ensuring only authorized voters can participate (using Anonymous Credentials).
- **Coercion Resistance**: Strategies to prevent voters from being forced to vote a certain way (e.g., fake credentials or re-voting options).

## 2. Censorship Circumvention
Ensuring that information remains accessible and communication remains free even in restrictive environments.
- **Tor**: Hides the destination of traffic, making it hard for censors to block specific sites.
- **Bridges**: Unlisted Tor entry nodes that help users connect when the main Tor nodes are blocked.
- **Steganography**: Hiding the existence of a message within another, seemingly innocent, file.

## 3. Anonymous Petitions
Allowing citizens to sign formal requests to authorities without fear of social or professional retaliation, using **Anonymous Credentials**.

## 4. The "No Backdoor" Principle
Privacy researchers strongly discourage adding "backdoors" for law enforcement. Any mechanism that allows authorized access to encrypted data inherently weakens the system, as it can be exploited by malicious actors or used to undermine democratic rights.
