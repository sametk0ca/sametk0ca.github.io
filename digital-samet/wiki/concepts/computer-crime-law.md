# Concept: Computer Crime Law

## Overview
Computer crime law focuses on criminalizing activities that target information systems or use them as a primary tool for illicit acts. The most significant international effort to harmonize these laws is the **Budapest Convention on Cybercrime**.

## Crimes Against Information Systems
The Budapest Convention identifies four core categories of crimes against the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of computer data and systems:

1. **Illegal Access**: Accessing the whole or any part of a computer system without right (often referred to as "hacking").
2. **Illegal Interception**: Intercepting non-public transmissions of computer data to, from, or within a computer system without right.
3. **Data Interference**: Damaging, deleting, deteriorating, altering, or suppressing computer data without right.
4. **System Interference**: Seriously hindering the functioning of a computer system by inputting, transmitting, damaging, deleting, deteriorating, altering, or suppressing computer data.

## Key Legal Concepts
- **"Without Right"**: A central element in defining these crimes. It excludes actions authorized by the owner, by law (e.g., search warrants), or by contract.
- **Intent**: Most computer crimes require proof of criminal intent (*mens rea*). Accidental or negligent actions usually do not fall under these specific criminal statutes.
- **De Minimis Exceptions**: Some jurisdictions exclude very minor or technical infractions from criminal prosecution to focus on significant harms.

## Related Crimes
- **Computer-related Fraud**: Causing loss of property to another by inputting, altering, or deleting computer data with fraudulent intent.
- **Computer-related Forgery**: Inputting, altering, or deleting computer data resulting in inauthentic data with the intent that it be considered authentic.
- **Content-related Offenses**: Including child pornography and copyright infringements.
