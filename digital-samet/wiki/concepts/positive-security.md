# Concept: Positive Security

## Overview
Traditional security often relies on "Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt" (FUD) to drive investment and compliance. **Positive Security** seeks to move beyond this by making security a credible and desirable proposition.

## From "Freedom From" to "Freedom To"
- **Traditional (Negative) Security**: Focuses on "freedom from" attacks, losses, and negative consequences.
- **Positive Security**: Focuses on "freedom to" engage in valued activities and cherish experiences securely. It enables, rather than restricts.

## Key Principles
1. **Credibility**: Security advice must be practical and actionable to be credible.
2. **Engagement**: Stakeholders should be involved in decision-making and delivery, feeling responsible for the organization's defense.
3. **Language**: Stop demonizing users as "The Weakest Link." This phrasing implicitly blames individuals for poorly designed systems.
4. **Negotiation**: Security solutions should be negotiated to meet the needs of all stakeholders, not just imposed top-down.

## Stakeholder Engagement Techniques
- **Projective Techniques**: Using drawings, collages, or physical modeling (e.g., Lego) to represent daily activities and identify root causes of insecure behavior.
- **Creative Security Engagements**: Encouraging users to reflect on their environment, emotions, and constraints when sharing information.
- **Productive Security**: Aligning security design with user tasks to reduce workload and increase organizational productivity.
