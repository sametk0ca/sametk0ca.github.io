# Concept: Adversarial Modeling Frameworks

## 1. Technical Models
- **Attack Trees**: Formalized visualization where the root is the goal and branches are the sub-goals and actions required. Includes 'OR' (alternative paths) and 'AND' (required steps) nodes.
- **Cyber Kill Chain**: A 7-step model (Reconnaissance to Actions on Objectives) used to identify opportunities for disruption (Detect, Deny, Disrupt, Degrade, Deceive).

## 2. Environmental Criminology
Applying established criminal theory to the digital space:
- **Routine Activity Theory**: Crime requires a motivated offender, a suitable target, and the absence of a capable guardian.
- **Rational Choice Theory**: Offenders make rational decisions based on risk vs. reward.
- **Pattern Theory**: Identifying "hotspots" (Crime Attractors, Generators, and Enablers) in cyberspace.
- **Situational Crime Prevention**: Reducing opportunities by increasing effort/risk and reducing rewards/provocations.
- **Crime Scripting**: Breaking down the sequence of steps in an offense (similar to kill chains but from a behavioral perspective).

## 3. Economic Modeling
- **Flow of Capital**: Identifying "Profit Centers" (where money enters) and "Support Centers" (services consumed by criminals).
- **Bottlenecks**: Finding points in the financial chain (e.g., cashing out) where law enforcement can intervene most effectively.

## 4. Attack Attribution
Determining who is behind an attack based on:
- **Tactical Layer**: The "How" (technical signatures, exploits used).
- **Operational Layer**: The "What" (architecture, infrastructure).
- **Strategic Layer**: The "Why" (motivation, geopolitical context).

*Note: Attribution is difficult because actors can use "False Flags" or reuse exploits captured from other states.*
